How to build your use case

Introduction

This information page explains how to design a use case. First of all, there are a lot of resources involved in designing a use case. It is quite a task to convert a problem must be into a policy question and then translate the question into data-items. During this translation process privacy, ethics and security play a large role. In this process the worlds of technology, legislation and policy are connected. To do so, different roles and persons are required. Make sure that at least a pilot manager, policy officer, mobility provider(s), data analyst, privacy and security officer are involved in the design of the use case. Good luck!

Step 1: Define the problem

Templates: Use case definition canvas

Roles: pilot manager, policy officer, data analyst, mobility operator

Choose a real problem that is present in your city and that relates to shared mobility or multimodal travel. Define your problem as accurately as possible. The problem must be urgent, specific and clear.

  1. Who is experiencing the problem?
  2. Why is it a problem?
  3. What does the world look like without the problem?

You can use the 'Use case definition canvas' to guide you in describing the problem.

If your problem is loud and clear, investigate what assumptions underlie this problem. Be specific. Answers to the questions below are the assumptions that exist for the problem in question.

  1. what kind of events are causing your problem?
  2. By what behavior, by whom, and why is the problem caused do you think?

If you want to solve the problem in your city, the assumptions underlying the problem must first be tested. Are these events really the cause of the problem? Only when you know the causes of the problem, and they have been proven, can tailor-made effective solutions be formed.

Always check with the mobility provider(s) in question to see if the core of the problem corresponds to the mobility providers' experience and of their users. Include the mobility provider at the beginning of the process. This will save you a lot of resistance and ensure good cooperation which is essential for the data exchange.

The metrics

Based on the validated problem and assumptions, you can draw up a hypothesis - what your current understanding of the root of the problem is. Translate the assumptions together with your data analyst into measurement indicators, also known as metrics. Make sure the metrics are measurable in numbers.  Mobility data is quantitative, meaning it is information that can be captured  in figures and is based on facts. The metrics are about the actual use of mobility services.  Fill in the metrics in the 'Use case definition canvas'. [a]

Mobility data do not answer questions about motivation or travel behavior, or other qualitative matters.  Mobility data is, therefore, not a solution in itself, but rather a means to identify problems and monitor behavior.  Data is a tool, not the holy grail. An outcome of data analysis could be that more sources are needed to map the problem properly.

It is important to be aware that numbers in themselves are objective, but that the type of data you measure and the metrics are shaped by us and will therefore always be subjective. Therefore, always keep questioning the data and do not label it as "reality. Thus, applying mobility data can be a starting point for (policy) research, but it is certainly not the finish line or a ready-made answer to your research question.

Step 2: Build your use case

Template: 'Use case definition canvas' 

Roles: pilot manager, policy officer, data-analyst

Get the  structure right

Now that you know what assumptions require testing, it is time to translate the metrics into specific data items. These data items are generated by the mobility assets that travel through the city.  Use the ‘data standards’ information page[b] to discover which data standard fits your data need. Data standards are formats that enable the data sharing between city and operator and therefore need to match the modalities and data needs of the city. The information page on ‘data standards’ will clarify and simplify this rather tough information and task.  

This translation from metrics to data standards is already built into the use case store. The use case store also incorporates data privacy and ethics by design. To ensure that data privacy is properly covered by your own design, it is important that the purpose reasoning is strong. Use the structure below to check if the purpose reasoning fits the data items you have come up with. If this structure can be filled in logically, then you are good to go. The paragraph below is included in the  'Use case definition canvas'. Fill in the paragraph and add the data items and selected standard.  

As a.... [function]

I would like to .... [what you want to know]

To ...... [the decision you want to make in the future]

The advantages for the city are ...... [what does the world look like without the problem]

Purpose reasoning is just one aspect of data privacy.  To be fully compliant with privacy and ethics by design all Data Protection Principles and ethical values related to data collection need to be taken into account. These pillars are used to respect all interests and rights of data subjects. Get your privacy officer on board during the next steps. Trust me, you're going to be very happy with the presence!

The use case store

The use case store is a digital space where all already performed use cases are bundled. For the use cases in the store, the privacy and ethics by design method has already been applied, and recommended data standards are given. Therefore these use cases have an excellent Data Protection Score.

In this 'webshop', you can check  if your use case objective is on offer. You can make your own selection of use cases, put them in the basket,  and pay. The receipt states exactly what your data protection score is and what data items, data standards, and agreements you need to perform the analysis. Using use case(s) from the store is a ‘shortcut’ option. You have been so brave to take the ‘independent route’ of designing your own use case. Congratulations!

Option 1 – The shortcut:  Go to the use case store and see if your use case has already been designed for you. You can skip the four steps of the independent route.  

Option 2 – The independent route: if you cannot find your problem and use case, go through the following three steps of the use case development process yourself.

Step 3: The independent route

Four steps of Ethics and Privacy by design 

Roles: pilot manager, policy officer, data-analyst, privacy officer

Three steps of Ethics and Privacy by design

  1. Refine data-items with privacy and ethics by design

In the previous step, you filled in your use case canvas and translated the problem into data items with strong purpose reasoning. Now it is time to review the data items with the privacy and ethics by design checklist[c]. The checklist consists of  Data Protection Principles and ethical values, and with those, we sharpen one more time at the data items until we are sure there is a perfect fit for purpose and the interests and rights of the data subjects are taken into account. After applying the checklist, the final data items are determined and we can go through the last step of the use case design.

  1. Determine if personal data is used and score your use case

Use the  Grading system[d] to scre your use case design on data protection and identify if (indirectly identifiable) personal data is used. Make sure that your use case has a score no higher than B, otherwise you are breaching privacy law. If personal data is used go to our CDS-M magazine[e] and download the obligated assessments and agreements for data sharing.

The Ethical leaflet (optional)

If you want to pay extensive attention to Ethics, you can make an Ethical leaflet[f] of your project. When creating the Ethical Leaflet, the side effects of the data exchange come to the surface. These side effects can be positive and negative, they allow you to improve the design of data processing morally. This step is optional.

  1. Add all information to the canvas and finalize your use case design

Add your Data Protection Score to your use case canvas, together with the category of data: non-personal or (indirectly identifiable) personal data. With this information, your use case is officially finalized and you can go to the magazine for the implementation phase.

The implementation phase

Step 3 Privacy assessment, Step 4 Security , Step 5 Legal arrangements, Step 6 Data exchange and Step 7 Evaluation.

Roles: pilot manager, policy officer, data analyst, privacy- and security officer, mobility operator

If the data items fall in the category of personal data go to the magazine and perform the obligated assessments[1] to determine what privacy and security measures should be taken prior to data sharing and analysis. Explanation and templates of these assessments and required agreements are included in the magazine. Step 3 to 7 of the implementation phase of your use case are explained in the magazine.

Evaluation

After a maximum period of one year, the exchange of data and the application of the data should be evaluated. Are the assumptions tested against the established metrics? What is the outcome of this? Is the research question answered? What has been learned? An evaluation template [g]is included in the magazine.

If the evaluation shows that the focus has changed or is about to change, this means that there is a different purpose limitation. This implies that the toolbox cycle must be re-executed, because the use case definition has changed and with it the content of the risk analyses and agreements as well. Follow the 7 steps again and update the DPIA and BIO test.

[1] A Data Protection Impact Asessment is required under GDPR law if (indirectly identifiable) personal data is shared and processed.
