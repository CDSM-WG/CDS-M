Grading System Use Cases

Our grading system is based on two stages of grading.

Stage 1 Privacy Score 

The first stage is determining if personal data is processed under GDPR regulations. This is reviewed per data specification included in your selection of use cases. Your selection in the ‘basket’ gets labeled containing or non-containing personal data; this is the privacy score of your shopping bag. If your shopping bag contains personal data this implies privacy and security checks and agreements are obligated before processing. The specific requirements will then be added to the implementation instructions on your receipt when checking out.

The definition of ‘personal data’ given in the GDPR Article 4, is the following:

‘Personal data’ means any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person (‘data subject’); an identifiable natural person can be identified, directly or indirectly, in particular by reference to an identifier such as a name, an identification number, location data, an online identifier or to one or more factors specific to the physical, physiological, genetic, mental, economic, cultural or social identity of that natural person.

Looking back at the GDPR’s definition, there is a list of identifiers: “a name, an identification number, location data, an online identifier.”. The GDPR provides several examples in Recital 30; vehicle/device IDs are also possible identifiers. Vehicle and device IDs are often linked to the location or trip of a shared vehicle. If so, the data is indirectly identifiable personal data. Individual routes without IDs but with two or more points are still identifiers. The routes are often so unique per user that they are like an identifier in themselves. In other words, mobility data is often personal location data.

CDS-M STATEMENT: Personal location data carries information about assets in use that is directly or indirectly identifiable. For example, GPS point(s) linked to a user by an ID or number plate of an asset or a unique displacement formed by two or more GPS points.

Stage 2 Data Protection Score 

The second scoring phase assesses your unique selection of use cases as a whole and ensures Data Protection by Design. The scoring is done on the basis of the Data Protection Principles (DPPs) related to data collection written down in the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). The DPPs related to data collection guarantee that the correct amount of data is processed for the purpose reasoning and the data colection is lawful and fair.  

Article 5.1 of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) refers to the Data Protection Principles (DPPs). The controller must comply with the DDPs in the execution of the data processing.

The Data Protection Principles that relate to data collection are the three below[1]:

  1. Purpose limitation (DDP)
    The purpose of the processing must be determined in advance and described in as specific a way as possible. The design of the processing should be shaped on the basis of what is necessary to achieve the purposes.

  1. Data minimalization (DDP)
    Only data that is sufficient, relevant and limited to what is necessary for the purpose, will be processed. Check whether the goal can only be reached by this route. If there is another way in which less data is processed, this principle is violated.

  1. Lawfulness, fairness and transparency (DDP)
    The data must be processed on a     lawful basis.  In the case of CDS-M, this basis is usually 'in the public interest'. The processing of data must be proper, in other words necessary and proportionate, and transparent. Transparent processing respects the rights of data subjects; right of access, oblivion, rectification and completion, etc. The data subject must also be informed of the processing.

A fixed set of questions are asked to score with the DDPs:

  • Is the summary and the purpose of the use case(s) as specific as possible?

Summary of your use case: [fill in]

as a …. [your function]

I would like to …[what you want to measure]

In order to…[what you want to understand]

The definition of done is …[what you want to solve]

  • What data-items are collected? Are the data-items as minimalized as possible for the use case(s)?
  • Is the there a legal basis used for the use case(s)? Is the processing necessary and proportional? Are all rights of the data subject respected?

For further explanation of elaboration on the necessity and proportionality requirement see the checklist from the European Data Protection Supervisor Board. https://edps.europa.eu/sites/default/files/publication/20-01-28_edps_quickguide_en.pdf 

The Data Protection scores (selection of use-cases)

[a]

A- non-personal data

B- personal data & fully complies with purpose limitation & data minimization (read all fields are used optimally).

C– unnecessary personal data; when eliminating specific fields, the data is non-personal. Does not comply with purpose limitation & data minimization.

D- personal data and not fully compliant. The proper specification is used, but specific data fields must be eliminated when processing to comply with purpose limitations & data minimization (e.g. non-rotating IDs while this is not necessary). The surplus of data processed is not necessary nor proportional.  

E- a surplus of personal data or unlawful processing. Too many data items are requested for the purpose of data collection (e.g. the routes when you only need availability) or a too broad/extensive specification is used. The surplus of data processed is not necessary nor proportional.  Another reason for a score ‘E’ ca be that here is not a suitable legal basis or the rights of the data subject are not obliged by. Then the data is processed unlawfully.

Example scoring shared mobility use case:  

  • Use case check. Use case summary and purpose are described as specific as possible.
  • What data are collected?
  • Data items are the availability data of non-booked assets.
  • Are the data-items as minimalized as possible for the use case(s)?

Are the IDs rotated or removed? Fixed IDs are only needed if you need to know the start and endpoint of a ride, for general availability data of non-booked assets this is not necessary.

Are the IDs rotated or removed?

  • if yes, score A;
  • if not score C;
  • and if standards are used that share types of data more sensitive than availability data, you get a score E.
  • Legal basis check. Legal basis: public interest. Rights of data subject check.
  • All rights respected, score same level.  
  • If not in order, score shifts to an E.
