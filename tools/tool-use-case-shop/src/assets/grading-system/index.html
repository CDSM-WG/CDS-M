Grading System Use Cases

Introduction

Our grading system is based on two stages of grading.

  1. Privacy Score - The first stage is determining if the data processed for every single use case in your selection/basket is personal data under GDPR. If all use cases process non-personal data your selection is graded with a Data Protection Score of A. Step 2 is only required if personal data is processed.
  2. Data Protection Score - Secondly, the selection of use cases is assessed all together on the basis of the Data Protection Principles written down in the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). Article 5.1 of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) refers to the Data Protection Principles (DPPs). The controller must comply with the DDPs in the execution of the data processing.

The Data Protection Principles that relate to data collection are the three below:

  1. Purpose limitation (DDP)
    The purpose of the processing must be determined in advance and described in as specific a way as possible. The design of the processing should be shaped on the basis of what is necessary to achieve the purposes.

  1. Data minimalization (DDP)
    Only data that is sufficient, relevant and limited to what is necessary for the purpose, will be processed. Check whether the goal can only be reached by this route. If there is another way in which less data is processed, this principle is violated.

  1. Lawfulness, fairness and transparency (DDP)
    The data must be processed on a     lawful basis.  In the case of CDS-M, this basis is usually 'in the public interest'. The processing of data must be proper, in other words necessary and proportionate, and transparent. Transparent processing respects the rights of data subjects; right of access, oblivion, rectification and completion, etc. The data subject must also be informed of the processing.

The definition of ‘personal data’ given in the GDPR Article 4, is the following:

‘Personal data’ means any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person (‘data subject’); an identifiable natural person can be identified, directly or indirectly, in particular by reference to an identifier such as a name, an identification number, location data, an online identifier or to one or more factors specific to the physical, physiological, genetic, mental, economic, cultural or social identity of that natural person.

Privacy score[a] (data type)

As stated above, any information leading to either the direct or indirect identification of an individual is to be considered personal data under the GDPR.

Looking back at the GDPR’s definition, there is a list of identifiers: “a name, an identification number, location data, an online identifier.”. The GDPR provides several examples in Recital 30; vehicle/device IDs are also possible identifiers. Vehicle and device IDs are often linked to the location or trip of a shared vehicle. If so, the data is indirectly identifiable personal data. Individual routes without IDs but with two or more points are still identifiers. The routes are often so unique per user that they are like an identifier in themselves. In other words, mobility data is often personal location data.

CDS-M STATEMENT: Personal location data carries information about assets in use that is directly or indirectly identifiable. For example, GPS point(s) linked to a user by an ID or number plate of an asset or a unique displacement formed by two or more GPS points.

Privacy Score 1: non personal = non-personal data

Privacy Score 2: personal = indirectly identifiable personal data [b][c]

C– unnecessary personal data; when eliminating specific fields, the data is non-personal. Does not comply with purpose limitation & data minimisation.

D- personal data and not fully compliant. The proper specification is used, but specific data fields must be eliminated when processing to comply with purpose limitations & data minimisation

E- a surplus of personal data. Too many data items are requested for the purpose of data collection (e.g. the routes when you only need availability). A too broad/extensive specification is used.

Data Protection Principles used for scoring:

  1. Purpose limitation → The principle requires that any processing of personal data must be done for a specific, well-defined purpose and only for additional purposes that are compatible with the original purpose
  2. Data minimalisation → Only such data shall be processed as are “adequate, relevant and not excessive in relation to the purpose for which they are collected and/or further processed”.
  3. Lawfulness, fairness and transparency (DDP)
    The principle requires that data must be processed on a     lawful basis. The processing of data must be proper, in other words necessary and proportionate, and transparent. Transparent processing respects the rights of data subjects; right of access, oblivion, rectification and completion, etc. The data subject must also be informed of the processing.
  4. For further explanation of elaboration on the necessity and proportionality requirement see the checklist from the European Data Protection Supervisor Board. https://edps.europa.eu/sites/default/files/publication/20-01-28_edps_quickguide_en.pdf 
  5. There are more data protection principles[1], but those usually apply to the latter stages in the data processing/chain[2].
  6. EU publications ► Handbook on European data protection law – Privacy by design, page 122 (Link)

Elaboration scoring method:

  1. Do you want to know the data of non-booked assets?
  • If so, are the IDs rotated or removed? This data is only needed if you need to know the start and endpoint of a ride.
  • if yes, score A;
  • if not score C;
  • and if standards are used that share types of data more sensitive than availability data, you get a score E.
  1. Do you want to know the ride data of the assets; or data from booked assets?
  • Then check whether data about IDs or status changes are unnecessarily obtained.
  • is this the case, but is the correct standard being used? Then you get a score of D.  
  • if fields or standards are used that communicate more sensitive data you don't need, you get a score of E;
  • for example entire routes if only the endpoints are relevant.  
  • use as little data as possible to reach your purpose and use the right standard, then you’ll get a nice score of B.
