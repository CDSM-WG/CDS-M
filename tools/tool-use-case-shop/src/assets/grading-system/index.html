Grading System Use Cases

Our grading system uses two stages of grading.

Stage 1 Privacy Score 

The first stage is determining if personal data is processed under GDPR regulations. Your selection in the ‘shopping basket’ gets labeled containing or non-containing personal data; this is the privacy score of your shopping basket. If your use cases include personal data, you’ll have to perform privacy and security checks before processing. These checks and the required agreements are on your receipt, which you can download at check-out.

The definition of ‘personal data’ given in the GDPR Article 4 is the following:

‘Personal data’ means any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person (‘data subject’); an identifiable natural person can be identified, directly or indirectly, in particular by reference to an identifier such as a name, an identification number, location data, an online identifier or to one or more factors specific to the physical, physiological, genetic, mental, economic, cultural or social identity of that natural person.

Looking back at the GDPR’s definition, there is a list of identifiers: “a name, an identification number, location data, an online identifier.”. The GDPR provides several examples in Recital 30; vehicle/device IDs are also possible identifiers. Vehicle and device IDs are often linked to the location or trip of a shared vehicle. If so, the data is indirectly identifiable personal data. Individual routes without IDs but with two or more points are still identifiers. The routes are often so unique per user that they are like an identifier in themselves. In other words, mobility data is often personal location data.

CDS-M STATEMENT: Personal location data carries information about assets in use that is directly or indirectly identifiable. For example, GPS point(s) linked to a user by an ID or number plate of an asset or a unique displacement formed by two or more GPS points.

Stage 2 Data Protection Score 

The second scoring phase assesses your unique selection of use cases as a whole and ensures Data Protection by Design. The scoring is based on the Data Protection Principles (DPPs) related to data collection in the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). The DPPs related to data collection guarantee that the correct amount of data is processed for the purpose reasoning and that the data collection is lawful and fair.  

Article 5.1 of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) refers to the Data Protection Principles (DPPs). The controller must comply with the DDPs in the execution of the data processing.

The Data Protection Principles that relate to data collection are the three below[1]:

  1. Purpose limitation (DDP)
    The purpose of the processing must be determined in advance and described in as specific a way as possible. The processing design should be shaped based on what is necessary to achieve the purposes.

  1. Data minimalization (DDP)
    Check whether the method is necessary to reach the goal. Only data that is sufficient, relevant, and limited to what is necessary for the purpose will be processed. This principle is violated if there is another way in which less data is processed.

  1. Lawfulness, fairness, and transparency (DDP)
    The data must be processed on a     lawful basis.  In the case of CDS-M, this basis is usually 'in the public interest. Data processing must be proper, in other words, necessary, proportionate, and transparent. Transparent processing respects the rights of data subjects; right of access, oblivion, rectification, completion, etc. The data subject must also be informed of the processing.

A fixed set of questions are asked to score with the DDPs:

  1. Is the summary and the purpose of the use case(s) as specific as possible?

Summary of your use case: [fill in]

as a …. [your function]

I would like to …[what you want to measure]

In order to…[what you want to understand]

The definition of done is …[what you want to solve]

  1. What data-items are collected? Are the IDs rotated or removed? Fixed IDs are needed to know an individual ride’s coupled start and endpoint. For general availability data of non-booked assets, this is not necessary.

  1. Is there a legal basis for the use case(s)? Is the processing necessary and proportional? Are all rights of the data subject respected?

For further explanation of the necessity and proportionality requirement, see the European Data Protection Supervisor Board checklist. https://edps.europa.eu/sites/default/files/publication/20-01-28_edps_quickguide_en.pdf 

Data Protection Scores 

A
- non-personal data

B
- personal data & fully complies with purpose limitation & data minimization (read all fields are used optimally).

C
– unnecessary personal data; when eliminating specific fields, the data is non-personal. The selection of use cases does not comply with the purpose limitation & data minimization principles.

D
- personal data and not fully compliant. The proper specification is used, but specific data fields must be eliminated when processing to comply with purpose limitations & data minimization (e.g., non-rotating IDs while this is not necessary). The surplus of data processing is not necessary nor proportional.  

E
- an excess of personal data or unlawful processing. The excess of data processing is not necessary nor proportional. Too many data items are requested for the scope and purpose of data collection (e.g., the routes when you only need availability), and a too broad/extensive specification is used. Another reason for a score of ‘E’ can be that there is no suitable legal basis, or the data subject’s rights are not obliged. Then the data is processed unlawfully.
