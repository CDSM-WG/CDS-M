How to build your use case

  1. Describe the ‘Problem’
  • what is the problem in your city?
  • what does the world look like when the problem doesn’t exist?

The problem is the base of your use case. Your problem will also be your legal base to share data. Describing the problem carefully and specifically is, therefore, incredibly important.

  1. Describe the ‘Why’
  • why is it a problem? Why do we want to work on this?
  • What is the underlying cause?
  • what are your assumptions?

Assumptions function as our hypothesis in researching the problem. Establishing explicit assumptions is essential for evaluation. You will check the premises and analyze the data to find an answer to your research question. Evaluating will improve the data exchange and solve the problem.

  1. Describe the ‘What’
  • what do you need to know to understand the problem better?
  • what is your research question?
  • what do you need to measure?
  • translate your assumptions and research question into indicators.

Describe what information you are currently missing to solve the problem. Make sure the research question fits seamlessly with the information you need and is specific, objective, neutral and measurable. Make sure the information you want to collect tests your assumptions. The rule here is: less is more. Finally, translate the information into quantitative measurement indicators, data-items. Data sharing is about numbers and averages, not about the personal behaviors of travelers.

Tip: share this document with data analysts in your organization too. They can help you with the translation step from information to measurement indicators and data.

  1. Decide ‘How’
  • search for a data specification that seamlessly fits the data items specified in step 3.
  • take a look at ‘Browse standards’ for an overview of often used specifications.
  • pick a data specification with the highest data protection potential.

Make sure your use case has the highest possible data protection level. Do so by taking into account data protection principles. All use cases in the store are labeled that way. You can find extensive information on our privacy by design approach in our CDS-M magazine[a] or a short overview on the information page ‘How to score a use case’.[b] 

  1. and describe the ‘Who’
  • what are your stakeholders?
  • what are the benefits of data sharing to (1) the city, (2) the mobility operator, and (3) your team?

In this step, you describe which stakeholders are involved in your data exchange. Data processing can have different effects on all stakeholders. It is crucial to check holistically if the citizen has a central focus in the design. Cooperation with mobility providers is essential in exchanging data, so consider their perspective.
